Historical performance

Top line

Revenue

Revenue shows how much business the company generated before expenses. A rising revenue line can indicate growth, but it should not be read alone.

Compare it with net income. If revenue rises but profit does not, the company may have cost pressure, weak margins, or heavy reinvestment.

Profitability

Net income

Net income shows the profit left after expenses, interest, taxes, and other costs. It is usually a stronger quality signal than revenue alone.

Stable or growing net income may indicate better operating discipline. Sharp drops may reflect business cycles, one-time charges, or margin weakness.

Balance sheet

Total assets

Total assets show the size of the company’s balance sheet. Asset growth may indicate expansion, acquisitions, or higher capital intensity.

More assets are not automatically better. Read this together with ROA, debt, equity, and net income to see whether the assets are productive.

Valuation and quality indicators

How to read valuation and quality indicators

These ratios are simple financial indicators calculated from the prepared local dataset. They are not live market signals. Green, yellow, and red are educational thresholds used to make the numbers easier to read.

P/EPrice compared with earnings. Lower can mean cheaper, but very low can also reflect risk.
P/BPrice compared with book value. Lower can mean cheaper relative to accounting equity.
ROEProfit generated from shareholder equity. Higher usually means better profitability.
ROAProfit generated from total assets. Higher means assets are used more efficiently.
D/EDebt compared with equity. Lower usually means less balance-sheet leverage.
Current RatioCurrent assets compared with current liabilities. Higher means stronger short-term liquidity.
Gross MarginShare of revenue left after direct costs. Higher means stronger product or service margin.

Sector and peer context

How peer comparison works

InvestIQ first looks for companies in the same industry. If there are not enough peers, it expands to the same sector. The table shows comparable companies. The bar chart shows metric values. The radar chart converts each metric to a 0-100 score, where a higher score is always better.

For radar scoring, higher is better for ROE, ROA, Current Ratio, and Gross Margin. Lower is better for P/E, P/B, and Debt-to-Equity. This makes unlike metrics easier to compare visually.

How the valuation signal works

The classifier is trained locally from the prepared historical dataset. It learns labels based on sector-relative valuation rules, not from live market prices or analyst opinions. The verdict is therefore an educational signal, not an investment recommendation.

Confidence is the model probability for the selected verdict. Feature importance shows which ratios were most influential during model training.

Educational backtest

Simple historical simulation

How to read this backtest

This backtest is a simple learning tool. It answers one question: what would have happened if a basic value-and-quality rule had been applied to the selected tickers during the selected historical period?

Buy rule A ticker is bought when the latest available annual fundamentals show P/E below 15 and ROE above 15%. This means the company appears reasonably priced and profitable based on the local dataset.
Sell rule A position is sold after 252 trading days, roughly one trading year, or earlier if the selected end date is reached.
Capital allocation Initial capital is split equally across tickers that trigger the buy rule. Tickers that do not trigger the rule are shown as skipped.
Data limitation Prices are daily, but fundamentals are annual. InvestIQ uses the latest available annual fundamentals at or before the trade year. This is realistic enough for learning, but it is not a professional trading engine.

The result should be read as an educational historical simulation, not as a forecast, trading signal, or investment recommendation.
